As AI systems get deployed in high-stakes domains like medicine, law, and finance, one question keeps coming up: why did the model make that decision? This is the core problem that Explainable AI (XAI) tries to solve.

This post is based on a research paper by Arrieta et al. published in Information Fusion (Elsevier), which provides one of the most comprehensive surveys of XAI concepts, taxonomies, and challenges. Here are the key ideas.

The Black Box Problem

Earlier AI systems — rule-based, decision trees — were easy to interpret. You could trace exactly why a decision was made. But modern Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have hundreds of layers and millions of parameters, making them effectively black boxes.

This matters because:

  • In precision medicine, doctors need more than a binary prediction — they need reasoning
  • In regulated industries (finance, legal), decisions must be justifiable and auditable
  • There is a fundamental trade-off between performance and transparency — the more powerful the model, the harder it is to explain

There is an emerging consensus: humans are reluctant to adopt techniques that are not directly interpretable, traceable, and trustworthy.

Terminology: Getting the Definitions Right

The paper makes an important distinction between terms that are often used interchangeably:

  • Understandability — can a human understand how the model works, without needing its internals explained?
  • Interpretability — the ability to explain or provide meaning in understandable terms (a passive property of the model)
  • Explainability — an active characteristic: any action or procedure taken to clarify a model's internal function
  • Transparency — a model is transparent if it is understandable by itself

The key insight: explainability depends on the audience. A technical explanation sufficient for an ML engineer may be meaningless to a doctor or a judge. The paper defines XAI as:

Given a certain audience, explainability refers to the details and reasons a model gives to make its functioning clear or easy to understand.

Why Do We Need Explainability?

Interpretability improves a model's real-world viability for three core reasons:

  1. Bias detection — helps detect and correct bias in training data
  2. Robustness — highlights potential adversarial perturbations that could flip predictions
  3. Causality assurance — guarantees that only meaningful variables influence the output

What Purposes Does XAI Serve?

The paper identifies several goals that motivate XAI research:

  • Trustworthiness — confidence that a model will act as intended (though a trustworthy model is not necessarily explainable on its own)
  • Causality — finding causal relationships among data variables, not just correlations
  • Transferability — understanding a model well enough to reuse its knowledge for other problems
  • Informativeness — giving users enough information to relate their decisions to the model's output
  • Confidence — ensuring stability and robustness before trusting interpretations
  • Fairness — visualizing what relations affect a result, enabling ethical analysis
  • Accessibility — helping non-technical users engage with ML models
  • Privacy awareness — understanding what data the model has captured internally to prevent privacy breaches

How: Transparent Models vs. Post-hoc Techniques

The paper draws a fundamental distinction between two approaches to explainability:

1. Transparent Models (Interpretable by Design)

These models have three levels of transparency:

  • Simulatability — can a human mentally simulate the model? Simple linear models qualify; complex rule systems do not
  • Decomposability — can each part (input, parameter, calculation) be individually explained?
  • Algorithmic transparency — can the user understand the full process from input to output? (e.g., a linear model's error surface can be reasoned about)

2. Post-hoc Explainability (Explaining Black Boxes After Training)

For models that are not interpretable by design, these techniques add explainability after the fact:

  • Text explanations — generating natural language or symbolic descriptions of model behavior
  • Visual explanations — dimensionality reduction and visualization techniques to show model behavior
  • Local explanations — explaining specific predictions rather than the whole model (e.g., LIME)
  • Explanations by example — extracting representative data examples that capture the model's internal relationships
  • Explanations by simplification — rebuilding a simpler model that approximates the original while being more interpretable
  • Feature relevance — computing relevance scores for each variable to show which features matter most

The Road to Responsible AI

The paper concludes with the concept of Responsible AI — a methodology for deploying AI at scale with fairness, explainability, and accountability at its core. The key principles:

  • Fairness, accountability, and privacy must be considered alongside performance
  • Explainability and privacy/security should be designed in together, not bolted on
  • There are real tensions between explainability and confidentiality — explaining a model's internals to unauthorized parties can compromise data privacy

XAI is not just a technical challenge — it is a prerequisite for AI systems that society can trust, audit, and hold accountable.
